Problem Solving

A Time When I Was Blocked

I was really stuck on a challenge called "Super FlipFlop" where you need to write a function returns "flip", "flop", or "flipflop" based on the number passed to it. At first I tried Googling for similar examples, but all the examples that I found were not similar enough. I felt frustrated and a bit like a failure. I had to steer myself away from black and white thinking such as "I'm just not cut out for coding".

I ended up reaching out to a facilitator for help, who gave me a video which went through the process of completing the challenge. By the end of the video, I still felt slightly confused, but not nearly as confused as I was during the challenge. I learned that for me, the best way of learning something is to see it be done in front of me, then for me to have a go at a similar example. I also need to remind myself to reach out for help sooner.


A Time When I Solved a Problem

I was stuck on a challenge where I needed to write a function that found Scooby Doo, in amongst an array of objects. I was struggling to find the best way to start my function, even though I knew the steps I needed to perform. Googling wasn't really working, as none of the examples I found really helped me. Again, I had to take small breaks when I felt myself getting frustrated, and I had to repeat my mantra ("a problem to solve, not a reason to quit") to keep myself going.

After realising in previous challenges that I really needed to see the code being written once or twice to see how it worked, I found a YouTube video where someone wrote out a similar function. I was then able to solve my own problem using their logic. I learned that in many functions, I need to have a way to store my result, so that it can be used properly. YouTube is probably going to be an important problem-solving resource for me moving forward.


A Time When I Was Hesitant to Ask for Help

I hesitated a lot to ask for help during sprint 4. It is my natural inclination to believe that I can solve a problem on my own, and that asking for help can "look bad". I understand that logically, this perspective is completely unhelpful and is damaging to my future success, so I am attempting to re-train that inclination over time.


My Confidence with Problem Solving Techniques

I am least confident in the techniques of pseudocode, rubber ducky, and console.log. I need a lot more practice with pseudocode and console.log in particular, because I haven't used them much.

I am more confident in the techniques of Googling and trying things until something works, although in retrospect, these techniques have been way less helpful to me overall. I am in the process of training myself to ask for help more readily when I need it.
