Neuroplasticity and the Growth Mindset

How does understanding neuroplasticity benefit people?

Understanding the principles of neuroplasticity can help people alleviate mental illnesses, overcome damages to the brain, perform better in school, improve memory, and learn new skills later on in life when it is seemingly more difficult to do so.


What are some ways to increase your neuroplasticity?

Movement and learning new skills through dextrous hobbies seem to be two of the best ways to improve neuroplasticity. Repetitive practice builds new neural pathways in the brain.


How I will engage with the principles of neuroplasticity to benefit my learning.

I will need to keep up with my daily walks with the dog and do a big hike once a week as my exercise. Although I do not currently have a dextrous hobby, I will look into either a sport (like badminton) or crafting hobby that will include learning a range of new skills.

Brian Greene hosted an interesting program with neuroscientists to explore the benefits of neuroplasticity.


What is the 'growth mindset'? Why is it relevant?

We tend to have a bias towards believing that natural talent, ability, and inherited circumstances are what determine our success or failure. This bias is often reinforced through society and culture. Many people then defeat themselves before they have even had the chance to succeed. Understanding that the mind can grow, change, and adapt to overcome challenges in ways previously thought impossible can help us all succeed.

A YouTube video I found useful on the topic of adaptability and the growth mindset was created by Dr. K. at HealthyGamerGG.


How I will integrate a growth mindset into my learning journey.

When considering how to integrate the growth mindset into my studies, I thought about the importance of how I think about challenges, and the behaviour I exhibit when faced with challenges.

I need to readily pay attention to my own thoughts, most importantly when I am struggling or am frustrated with a challenge. My natural reaction is to try solve the problem but when I can't do that instantly, I get frustrated and give up. I need to practice acknowledging how I feel, and then work to reframe my natural inclination to reflexively give up.

Cognitive flexibility will be crucial to success in my studies. My mantra will be "not a reason to quit, but a problem to solve". Keeping this in mind, and doing it in practice, will help me overcome challenges that in the past would've defeated me.

I will also need to be aware of black and white thinking, such as "I will never be able to solve this problem", or "I will never make a good developer". Catching myself in the moment of frustration, taking a small break and getting some fresh air when I need to, practicing mindfulness, and reframing the problem to solve will help me keep going.
